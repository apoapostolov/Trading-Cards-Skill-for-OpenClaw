# Card Grading Research — PSA & Modern Condition Standards

## PSA 1-10 Grading Scale

Based on PSA's published standards and community analysis (2024-2026).

### Grade Descriptions

| Grade | Name | Centering (F/B) | Key Characteristics |
|-------|------|-----------------|---------------------|
| **10** | Gem Mint | 55/45, 75/25 | Virtually perfect. Four sharp corners, clean edges, pristine surface. The pinnacle. |
| **9** | Mint | 60/40, 75/25 | One minor flaw allowed: very slight wax stain on reverse, minor printing imperfection, or slightly off-white borders. |
| **8** | NM-MT | 65/35, 75/25 | Appears Mint at first glance. Very slight wax stain, slightest fraying at one or two corners, minor printing imperfection, slightly off-white borders. |
| **7** | NM | 70/30, 75/25 | Slight surface wear, subtle corner/edge fraying, minor scuffing. Looks great overall. |
| **6** | EX-MT | 80/20, 75/25 | Visible surface wear or printing defect. Very light scratch on close inspection. Slightly graduated corner fraying. Minor focus issues. |
| **5** | EX | 80/20, 90/10 | Noticeable wear. Small crease possible. Light staining. Surface scratching. Rounded corners. |
| **4** | VG-EX | 85/15, 90/10 | Heavy wear. Multiple creasing. Noticeable staining. Significant corner rounding. |
| **3** | VG | 90/10, 90/10 | Very heavy wear. Creasing. Staining. Much corner rounding. Poor focus. |
| **2** | GD | 90/10, 90/10 | Accelerated corner rounding. Obvious surface wear. Scratching, scuffing, light staining. May have several creases. |
| **1** | PR-FR | 90/10, 90/10 | Poor condition. Extreme wear. Major creasing, heavy staining, severe damage. |

## The Four Grading Categories

### 1. Centering
- Most objectively measured category
- PSA measures from the **worst point** of tilt
- Front centering is the primary concern; back is more lenient
- Modern cards tend to have better centering than vintage
- **Impact**: Can single-handedly knock a card from 10 to 8 or 9

### 2. Corners
- Most common defect preventing high grades
- "Dinging" — tiny chips off corner tips
- "Fuzzing" — microscopic fraying visible only under magnification
- "Soft corners" — slight rounding from handling or packaging
- **Bent corners** — the dreaded defect. A visible bend line almost always caps at PSA 7 or lower
- Vintage wax pack cards are particularly prone to corner wear

### 3. Edges
- Generally the **least important** of the four categories
- Chipping — tiny pieces of card stock missing from edges
- Whitening — white spots where the colored edge surface has worn away
- Notching — small indentations along the edge
- Modern cards with chrome/holographic finishes can show "silvering" on edges

### 4. Surface
- **Most difficult to evaluate** — many flaws only visible under specific lighting angles
- Print lines — factory defects that run across the card surface
- Roller dimples — small circular indentations from manufacturing
- Scratches — handling damage, sometimes only visible when tilted under light
- Staining — wax/gum stains (vintage), finger oils, environmental
- Loss of gloss — surface has become dull from age or handling
- Print defects — misregistration, color spots, "puddles" of incorrect color
- Some factory defects are **set-specific** — e.g., nearly all Neo Destiny holographic Pokémon cards show silvering

## Modern vs Vintage Grading Realities

### Modern Cards (2015+)
- Higher PSA 10 rates due to improved printing technology
- Typical submission distributions:
  - PSA 10: 15-25%
  - PSA 9: 35-45%
  - PSA 8: 20-30%
  - PSA 7: 5-10%
  - PSA 6: 2-5%
  - PSA 5 or lower: ~1-3%

### Vintage Cards (pre-1990)
- Much harder to get high grades
- PSA 10 is extremely rare (often <2%)
- PSA 7-8 is typically the "high-end" for most vintage
- Centering is the biggest grade-killer for vintage

### Our Virtual Cards
- Modeled as modern production quality
- Distribution weighted toward 7-9 range (the realistic bulk)
- Grade 10 should feel genuinely exciting (rare but achievable)
- Grade 5 or lower should be immediately noticeable

## Common Collector Observations

### What collectors notice casually (without loupe/magnification):
- Centering: obvious tilt or uneven borders
- Corners: visible rounding or dinging
- Surface: scratches, print lines in certain light
- Edges: chipping or whitening (usually least noticed)

### What only professionals catch:
- Micro-printing imperfections
- Subtle focus registration issues
- Micro-surface scratches (need angled light)
- Factory roller marks
- Slight off-whiteness of borders
- Print dots or "fisheyes"

### What collectors say when inspecting cards:
- "Looks like a 10 to me" (then gets a 8 😅)
- "Corners look sharp"
- "Little off-center on the left"
- "Clean surface"
- "Got a ding on the top right"
- "Print line across the back"
- "Glossy, no scratches"
- "One soft corner"

## Value Impact by Grade

| Grade Range | Price Multiplier (relative to PSA 8) |
|-------------|--------------------------------------|
| 10 | 1.3x - 1.5x |
| 9 | 1.1x - 1.2x |
| 8 | 1.0x (baseline) |
| 7 | 0.75x - 0.9x |
| 6 | 0.55x - 0.75x |
| 5 | 0.35x - 0.55x |
| 4 or lower | 0.15x - 0.35x |

For high-end parallels (Superfractors, 1/1s), the grade multiplier is even more extreme — a PSA 10 Superfractor can be worth 2-3x a PSA 8.

- $0–$500 → Value tier ($28/card, slow)

Bulk (Collector's Club) saves ~$6/card but requires 20+ cards and annual membership ($149/yr).

### PSA vs SGC vs BGS (Quick Cost Snapshot)
- **PSA**: Commands strongest resale premiums. Fees $28–$600/card. Default for liquidity.
- **SGC**: Often cheaper, faster for vintage. Slabs gained respect in recent years. ~$20–$250/card.
- **BGS/CGC**: Niche use cases. BGS sub-grades are valued by some collectors. ~$25–$300/card.
